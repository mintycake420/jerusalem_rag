**INTRODUCTION TO THE ASSIZES OF THE HIGH COURT**

The judicial institutions of a people are the faithful mirror of its ideas, its customs, and its interests. One must not be surprised, therefore, if a body of laws never perishes entirely, and if, after having lost the authority which it once enjoyed, it remains yet for historians, publicists [writers on public law], and jurisconsults [legal experts] the object of profound study and the source of precious instruction. All systems of legislation may become, through the analysis of their character and the comparative examination of their contrasts and analogies, the subject of the most curious research; but there are two, above all others, which shall never cease to captivate, by different titles, the attention of men whose minds are directed toward the investigation of those principles which serve to govern societies, or to whom belongs the care of applying those principles: we speak of Roman legislation and Feudal legislation. 

Rome, having reached the decline of her power, and seeing the Empire she had founded fall beneath the onslaught of the Barbarians, enclosed the elements of eternal wisdom within a code of laws which saved, and would yet save again, the civilization of the world. Emerging from the midst of ruins, Feudalism grew in but a few moments, laid the foundations of a formidable government, and gave birth to a society animated, brilliant, and warlike, the instincts of which, after so many centuries and revolutions, still live in our midst.
