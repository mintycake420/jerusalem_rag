...all the vassals of the rest of Europe held their fiefs. For the Norman lords, this was but natural; as for the Anglo-Saxon lords, they were in no position to impose conditions upon the favors of their master. This methodical enfeoffment [the formal granting of land and rights in exchange for service] of an entire country, which was accomplished without obstacle or upheaval, provided Europe with a striking lesson. 

From that time forth, it was manifest that feudalism had not been fashioned for sedentary peoples; that such nations, when subjected to the feudal regime, must needs turn against one another that military ardor with which they were inflamed, should they find no occasion to pour out this surplus of vigor abroad. The enfeoffment of England demonstrated, moreover, that feudalism could bring forth, at a single stroke, solid and vigorous governments, and reproduce itself with greater facility than it had first been established. Such lessons are not soon erased from the minds of nations; and thus, thirty years after the conquest of England, all Europe arose and, cross in hand, transported and caused its institutions to flourish in the midst of peoples who were alien to it in religion, in customs, and in tongue. In truth, the example set by William the Conqueror had been a mere trial, attempted within sight of the coasts of France upon a land of small extent and little peril; the Crusades, by contrast, gave the fullest development to that element of conquest which feudalism harbored.

Political interests had no less a share than the religious spirit in the wars undertaken for the deliverance of the Holy Land.
