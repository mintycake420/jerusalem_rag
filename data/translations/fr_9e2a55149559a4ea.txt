The Crusaders, in traversing Asia Minor, had founded two Christian states: the Principality of Antioch and the County of Edessa. It was of great import that these two Christian colonies—which were situated between Constantinople and Jerusalem and ensured the safety of land communications between Syria and Europe—should not be abandoned to themselves nor left exposed to fall once more into the hands of the Muslims. The Crusaders, faithful to the traditions of their homeland, imitated in the East that which had been done in France upon the extinction of the Carolingian dynasty (the Frankish line of kings preceding the Capetians). Syria became a principality under the name of the Principality of Jerusalem, from which held—as great fiefs (*feudal land holdings*)—the Principality of Antioch and the County of Edessa; later, they detached Tripoli and its territory from the Principality of Jerusalem to make of it a fourth great fief of the crown. 

The union of these four lordships formed the Kingdom of Jerusalem. The king was thus king and prince of Jerusalem, even as Hugh Capet had been king and duke of France; and so that the resemblance might be complete, the King of Jerusalem surrounded himself with great officers of the crown, whose titles and functions recalled exactly the designations and duties of the officers who composed the court of the King of France. It is needless to dwell further upon the political organization of the Kingdom of Jerusalem; it suffices to know that the constitution of this new State was modeled upon that of the Kingdom of France, and that in a matter so grave, the Crusaders had the prudence to attempt no innovations.
