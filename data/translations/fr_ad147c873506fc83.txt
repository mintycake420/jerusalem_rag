The Barbarians, shortly after their settlement in the Gauls [the Roman provinces of Gaul], drafted their code of laws under the influence of this necessity; and these laws, though obscure and imperfect, were nonetheless sufficient to maintain, for a long space of time, the victors and the vanquished in the relative positions that fortune had assigned them.

The conquest of England by William the Bastard is, in the history of feudalism, one of the events most worthy of consideration; for this memorable fact provides, better than any other, the measure of the strength which feudal institutions then possessed. The fleet which set out on the 30th of September, 1066, from Saint-Valery, carried feudalism fully armed, and was to resolve, upon the opposite shore, this grave question: whether feudal principles, though powerless to found calm and orderly societies, were at least an instrument of conquest. 

William the Bastard, after the victory at Hastings, found himself the sovereign arbiter of a land where there reigned laws and a political regime that had nothing in common with feudalism; for if one may discover, in the Anglo-Saxon customs of the eleventh century, a sort of analogy with the customs of feudalism, such an analogy is vague and uncertain, arising merely from the necessary relationship that exists between the institutions of contemporary peoples who dwell as neighbors. William had been born and had lived in the atmosphere of feudal ideas; he owed his success to the energy of these ideas; he desired to make England the principal seat of his power and to richly establish there the companions of his fortunate enterprise, and nothing hindered his will: how then could one marvel that he had no other thought than to import into England the complete system of feudal institutions from Normandy, or rather, from France?
