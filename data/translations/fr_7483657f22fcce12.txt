**English Translation**

...[Godfrey], with the barons and the chiefs of the army, charged certain learned and experienced men to question the Crusaders of various European lands concerning the usages and customs of their countries. These commissioners devoted themselves to a profound inquiry and recorded the result of their researches in a book which they delivered unto the King. Godfrey presented this draft of a code to the Patriarch and the chiefs of the army, and, according to their counsel, "gathered from those writings that which seemed good to him, and therefrom made assizes [formal court decrees or laws] and usages which should be held and maintained and used in the kingdom of Jerusalem."¹ These laws, as the jurist from whom we borrow these details takes care to remark, were not intended solely for the native inhabitants (*regnicoles*) of the kingdom, but also for "all other manner of folk coming and going."²

There were two codes or charters: the one destined for the nobles, the other for the burgesses [the *Assises des Bourgeois*, or middle-class citizens]. Each assize or law was written separately in capital letters. The first letter of every law was illuminated with gold, and the codes bore the signature and the seal of the King, the Patriarch, and the Viscount of Jerusalem. John of Ibelin, who transmits these particulars to us, had not seen these collections of laws, which in his time no longer existed; but the importance which men never ceased to attach to this first promulgation of laws was so great, that all the particulars of this act were faithfully preserved in the memory of the jurists of the East.

These two codes received the name of the "Letters of the Sepulchre," because they were locked within a chest and placed in the treasury of the Church of the Holy Sepulchre, the most venerated place in the city of Jerusalem. This chest could not be opened, whether to consult or to modify the precious books it contained, save in the presence of nine persons, who were: the King or one of the great officers of the crown, two liege men [vassals bound by feudal duty] of the King, the Patriarch...
