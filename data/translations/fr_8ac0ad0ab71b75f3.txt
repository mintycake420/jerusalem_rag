Thus, the knowledge of that capital fact in the history of a political system—the fact by which all others are judged—was wanting to them. 

Far be it from us to seek to diminish the glory which so justly belongs to the writers of whom we speak; the imperfection we venture to remark in their labors was the fruit of the times. For it was then a question not of introducing some slight modifications into the ordering of society, but of renewing the very foundation of its institutions; of transforming, at one and the same time, the laws and the customs of **France**. How might one suppose that the natural voices of a divided people, full of passions and uncertain of their future, could bring to the spreading of their ideas that composure and stern equity which, in all ages and for all men, are qualities so difficult to attain?

Neither shall we reproach the **French** historians and **jurisconsults** [legal scholars and experts] for failing to delve deeply enough into the judicial antiquities of their land, or for remaining, in this regard, far inferior to the **jurisconsults** of **Germany**, who have let no law, custom, or tradition of that country pass without illuminating it with commentaries wherein breathe both a passion for knowledge and a love for the fatherland. The inferiority of the **French** writers in this matter arises less from the nature of their wit or their erudition than from political motives which, here again, have done injury to the merit of their works.
