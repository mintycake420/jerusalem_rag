...the position of the Crusaders in Syria; the subject which we treat obliges us to transport ourselves to the moment when the Christians, masters of Antioch, Edessa, and finally Jerusalem, set about organizing a government in the lands they had just conquered.

The leaders of the Crusaders, when they undertook this difficult task, did not enjoy that liberty of thought and action which is necessary to the legislator if his work is not to bear the stamp of circumstance and haste. Anxious at the spirit of disorder reigning within their armies, pressed to provide for the defense of establishments which, though scarcely founded, were already assailed from all sides, and divided among themselves by contrary views and rival ambitions, they had not the leisure to study the character of the political institutions of Europe in order to determine if it were fitting to transplant those institutions to the Orient. Pursued by necessity, these warrior-legislators, still covered in dust and blood, proclaimed a code of laws which reproduced the customs of the mother country, or rather of France; for from the very beginning of the conquest, that land assumed, over all of Eastern Christendom (*Chrétienté*), a political and moral ascendancy which for two centuries caused it to be regarded as the true metropolis of the Christian colonies of Asia.

It is this, it must be acknowledged, which gives such great historical value to the primitive laws of the Kingdom of Jerusalem. It is doubtless important to know how the Crusaders governed their colonies during the span of time that this fair and rich conquest remained in their power; but it is far more interesting to find, within the code of their laws, the elements of the legislation of Europe in the eleventh century—at an epoch when feudalism (*féodalité*) had reached, after so many efforts and struggles, its highest point of development.
