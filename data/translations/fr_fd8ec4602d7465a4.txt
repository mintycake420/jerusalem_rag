The Norman expedition to Sicily, that of the Burgundians to Portugal, and above all the conquest of England, stand as symptoms of that restless activity with which Europe was then travailed. It was amidst such circumstances that a lowly monk [Peter the Hermit], an instrument of Providence, raised his voice to summon the peoples of Europe unto a war of extermination against the Mahometan power, which held Asia beneath its yoke. He proffered unto them, as the lure best suited to entice and lead them forth, the deliverance of the Sepulchre of the Son of God and its purification from the presence of the Infidels; likewise, formidable cities to be stormed, populous and fertile provinces to be conquered, and lastly, an unbroken succession of hazards, perils, and feats of arms.

This summons spoke to the very heart of what the faiths, notions, and mores (*mœurs*) of Europe most ardently desired; thus were entire populations seen to forsake, without regret, the lands of their birth, to march toward the conquest of regions whose very situation was unknown to them. Lord and vassal, monk and serf, united by a singular purpose and by the same expectations, hastened with equal fervor toward those lands where they hoped to find that which Europe denied to their rightful desires or their worldly ambition. Such were the human considerations that moved the spirits of a multitude drawn from every estate of society, driven onward by a common instinct—the necessity to give free rein to a martial zeal which the state of Europe served only to stifle more with each passing day.

We shall not tarry to recount the events of the first expedition of the Crusaders into Syria; the matter of our discourse constrains us to pass to that moment when the Christians, having become masters of Antioch, Edessa, and lastly Jerusalem, set about the task of ordaining a government within the lands they had lately subdued.
