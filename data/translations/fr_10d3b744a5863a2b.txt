Yet one must not hide the truth that, in the history of legislations, it is sometimes impossible to return to the primal sources; then, one findeth himself constrained to grant unto simple traditions more trust than they rightfully deserve. Nature provideth unto the exact sciences a beacon of light that never waneth. In the historical sciences, contrariwise, if written and authentic documents be lacking, the historian or the publicist [a scholar of public law and political theory] findeth himself forced to fill this void by his own inductions, and thus uncertainty hovereth over the results he hath attained.

We possess but fragments of the Law of the Twelve Tables [the foundational legislation of ancient Rome], gathered with great pains from authors who wrote at least four centuries after the proclamation of said law; nevertheless, by following a most ingenious method of reconstruction, science hath succeeded in reviving this renowned legislation. Who knoweth all that is doubtful in a work of this kind? How may one grant full faith to those authors who have taken it as the foundation of their various systems? The Law of the Twelve Tables was engraved, as all men know, upon tablets of wood or ivory, and set forth in the Forum, before the rostra [the speaker's platform], so that every citizen might take knowledge thereof.
