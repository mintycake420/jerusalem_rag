...feudalism would not have been established, and France, for example, would have remained a monarchy, to the strengthening of which the aristocratic principle would have contributed in due measure; but the feudal association was formed precisely because the Crown could no longer ensure the public safety, and it was not to be supposed that the landholders, having armed and organized themselves, and having acquired, along with the consciousness of their own might, the certainty of their independence, would come humbly to place themselves under the tutelage of the Crown. 

Such an act of abnegation did not occur. Power is never lost, it is merely displaced; thus, in the era of which we treat, power resided no longer in the Crown, nor even in the high aristocracy; it was found within that multitude of military associations which had formed across all parts of the territory: therein lay the power and the future of society; thus do we see these associations grow rapidly and assume everywhere a regular form.

A single obligation originally united the grantee to the proprietor—or, to speak in the tongue of that age, the *seigneur* (lord) to the *vassal*—and this obligation consisted in marching to war under his command. Was it not natural that men who owed, by virtue of this bond, a tested fidelity to a chief whom they scrupulously obeyed in the most critical of straits, should take him as the ordinary arbiter of their disputes, in preference to the envoy of a prince with whom they held no direct relation, and who possessed not the power to make his justice respected? It has been said that the lords usurped the right of judgment; nothing is less exact: to take up a forsaken power and exercise it is not to usurp it.
