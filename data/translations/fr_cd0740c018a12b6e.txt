This collection of laws, we say in advance, promptly ceased to exist; but as all the efforts of the jurisconsults [legal scholars], whose writings we now publish, were aimed at reconstituting it and bringing it back to life, it is necessary to provide some clarifications regarding this primitive legislation of the Kingdom of Jerusalem.

The Holy City fell into the power of the Christians on July 23, 1099. After having given their first moments to the pious exaltation which animated their hearts, the Crusaders turned their thoughts to electing a leader. Their choice might long have wavered among several lords whose birth and recent exploits rendered them worthy of wearing a crown. Godfrey of Bouillon was chosen. This prince immediately occupied himself with constituting the Kingdom of Jerusalem and providing for the defense of the Christian establishments of the East. History attributes to him the honor of all that was done in this circumstance. Without wishing to rob him of this glory, one may consider that the political, military, and judicial organization of this new state was the collective work of all the leaders of the Crusade. The power of Godfrey was necessarily weak, as is all power which is the fruit of a recent election; and moreover, the only means of rendering solid the institutions then being created was to give them as a basis universal assent, rather than the particular ideas of the sovereign, however wise and profound one might suppose them to be.
