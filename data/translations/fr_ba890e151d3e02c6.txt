Between feudal law and the common law (*droit commun*), the struggle was nowhere so complete nor so marked by those incidents which render a combat of this nature instructive and curious, as it was in France. It is only within the history of this land that one may follow, with ease and method, the development of these two opposing principles, and embrace in a single glance the beginning, the many vicissitudes, and the final term of a struggle which was to decide the fate of ancient French society. A great multitude of historians, publicists, and jurisconsults [legal scholars] engaged themselves in a discussion that stirred so many interests, passions, and prejudices. Many among them brought to it only the tribute of their zeal; but several entered the lists [the arena of scholarly combat] with the weapons afforded by genius and science, and their writings shall ever be numbered among the titles of glory of our country.

It would therefore be natural to deem that feudal institutions, their history, the analysis of their character, and the appraisal of their merit, were henceforth an exhausted subject; that there exists no profound or ingenious insight which hath escaped the sagacity of Montesquieu; no obscure fact that the learning of the Du Canges, the Chantereaus, the Brussels, the Duboses, and so many others hath not illuminated; no question of feudal law for which Dumoulin, d'Argentré, Chopin, Loyseau, or Laurière do not provide, through their differing or contrary decisions, the means to resolve with facility.
