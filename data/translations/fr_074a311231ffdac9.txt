**Charlemagne** was occupied in staving off this destruction of central authority; but what could his profound views, his wise laws, and his brilliant victories avail against the momentum of the customs of his age? There remained but a single institution which yet recalled to the provinces the existence of a sovereign power: we speak of those military magistrates who, under the name of **counts** (*comtes*—provincial governors with judicial and military authority), commanded in the provinces and there oversaw the administration of justice. Charlemagne had applied himself to extending their authority and rendering it venerable, certain as he was of being able always to contain these officers within the bounds of duty; but upon his death the bond was broken, and the counts, by declaring themselves in their turn independent, and by placing themselves at the head of the **feudal associations** (*associations féodales*) which existed within their governments, rendered the isolation of the royalty complete. It was thus that this revolution was accomplished throughout all Europe; everywhere it was the result, not of the ambition of a few lords or the weakness of a few kings, but of the slow and painful fusion of the principles of civilization which the **Romans** and the **Barbarians** had spread across Europe.

If the facts have thus unfolded—if **feudalism** (*la féodalité*) is a work upon which several generations have labored with more or less activity, and which required no less than four centuries to be completed—can one be astonished to find nowhere the fundamental law, the primitive charter of this form of government? Who would have dictated such a law, since everyone, deceived by the slowness with which this great political transformation operated, perceived nothing which seemed to him new, nothing which could not be regarded as the consequence of that which had existed previously?
