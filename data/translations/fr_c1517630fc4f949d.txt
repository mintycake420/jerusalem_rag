...[The chest] could not be opened, whether to consult or to modify the precious books contained therein, save in the presence of nine persons, to wit: the King or one of the Great Officers of the Crown, two liege men (*hommes liges*) of the King, the Patriarch or the Prior of the Holy Sepulchre, two canons, the Viscount of Jerusalem, and two jurors of the Court of the Burgesses (*Cour des Bourgeois*).

One must account for a fact as strange as that which we have before our eyes, and explain the motives which moved these legislators to refuse all publicity and all true promulgation to a code of laws they had just drafted with such prudence and care. Were they unaware that a law which is not known to all cannot be enforced against any man, and that the judge must ever have the book of the law open before him when he dispenseth justice? Assuredly not. They possessed this elementary notion of right; yet the usages, or rather the prejudices of Europe, to which they were then still enthralled, led them to conceal in mystery the laws they had just inscribed, rendering their consultation difficult and their study impossible, thus preparing the way for the abrogation of the law by jurisprudence. 

In the eleventh century, there existed in no land of Europe any code of laws. The acts of that age which are graced with such a title were but simple regulations established by sovereigns or by lords for the governance of their private domains. The law, the only law truly recognized, was the simple will of the lords, who, surrounded by their vassals, pronounced their judgments in all liberty. The uniformity of these sentences, upon which usage necessarily exerted its influence, formed the custom (*coutume*).
