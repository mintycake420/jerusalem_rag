Grants of land, under the conditions aforementioned, were made by all those who had an interest in so doing. The Sovereign, the Holy Church, the lords of high estate or of lower degree, and landowners great and small, sought thereby to safeguardâ€”some their influence, authority, and riches; others the simple enjoyment of their possessions. Among all the benefits of this system of association, one must note the division of properties, which was a primary element of strength in primitive feudalism. These concessions were commonly granted for a fixed term; yet the grantees, well aware of how much the lords required their services, caused these holdings to be maintained for the duration of their lives, and often succeeded in establishing them hereditarily within their families. 

If we add that in the midst of this society, already so mingled by conflicting interests and customs, there existed certain proprietors who, by favor of fortunate circumstances, had managed to safeguard their domains without placing themselves under the protection of any man, and who henceforth enjoyed a complete independence, one shall have a general conception of the organization of property in all the States of Europe from the fifth unto the ninth century.
