Such is the system of institutions which was applied by the Crusaders to the lands they had just conquered. It is not necessary to take into account the era and the hazardous circumstances in which this system was founded to bestow praise upon it, and to affirm that it rested upon a profound knowledge of the interests and needs of both the victors and the vanquished. Had the duration of the Kingdom of Jerusalem depended upon the wisdom of the institutions granted to that State, it would have extended far beyond the limits accorded to it. Order, respect for the laws, and good harmony between the sovereign and his vassals did not always reign within the heart of Eastern Christendom; yet the development of those seeds of disorder which feudalism (*féodalité*) carried everywhere in its wake was contained therein as much as the national customs and the continual state of war in which the Crusaders lived would permit.

Having made known the political and judicial organization of the Kingdom of Jerusalem, we must now penetrate into the very heart of our subject and present an exposition of the principles upon which rested the codes of law given by Godfrey [of Bouillon] to this kingdom. But these codes have, unfortunately, perished under circumstances which we shall soon make known; and to offer an opinion regarding their character and merit, one must, while relying upon positive data, nevertheless yield to certain conjectures. One must therefore, in this delicate matter, be content to draw certain conclusions from facts that cannot be called into doubt.
