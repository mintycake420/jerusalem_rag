Yet, it was not so. The enfeoffment [the formal granting of lands as fiefs] of Syria was effected without any unrest; and the most rigorous equity must have presided over the partitioning of the cities, the castles, and all the domains of that rich country, since history has recorded no complaint nor any recrimination against an act so momentous and so difficult to execute.

The codification of the civil and criminal laws of the Kingdom of Jerusalem was undertaken immediately after the government had been constituted. It was executed—it is only just to acknowledge—with a sagacity and skill that the general spirit of the eleventh century, and the precarious position in which the Crusaders were placed, gave little reason to hope for. That warriors who, after three years of a life marked by great deeds and memorable victories, but also by excesses of every kind, should find the composure and deliberation necessary to debate and draft a code of laws—one which, for as long as the Christian colonies of the East endured, was regarded as a masterpiece of statesmanship and foresight—is a rare and noble example of wisdom. It demonstrated how much creative power was harbored within the feudal system.

Godfrey [of Bouillon], upon the counsel of the Patriarch of Jerusalem, the princes, the barons, and the leaders of the army, commissioned certain learned and experienced men to inquire of the Crusaders from the various lands of Europe concerning the usages and customs of their respective countries.
