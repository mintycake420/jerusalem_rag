Had the peoples of Europe dwelt under the dominion of peaceful institutions, which might have imparted to them a taste for a life calm, regular, and industrious, the voice of Peter the Hermit, though most powerful, could never have torn entire populations from their affections and their habits, nor cast them into all the hazards of the most perilous of expeditions. A man may sometimes abandon, to obey the impulse of an idea, his field, his hearth, and the objects of his tenderness; yet it is not so with whole nations, which in their great agitations obey causes more direct and positive.

Feudalism [the medieval social and military hierarchy of lords and vassals] had imposed upon Europe a purely military organization, and had made of it, so to speak, a vast camp. Everywhere one saw naught but lords and vassals, or rather, but chieftains and soldiers; everywhere resounded the din of arms and the cries of the combatants. Those struggles, so fierce, which accompanied the birth of the feudal regime, provided for several centuries sufficient fuel for this bellicose passion; but when the triumph of feudal principles had been universally acknowledged, a kind of truce was established among the peoples of Europe, and at the same time a state of malaise began to be felt. 

Society thirsted for strife and combat, and found, for its satisfaction, only private wars [feuds between rival nobles]—a deceptive image of the kind of emotions it craved. Throughout the eleventh century, one beheld the French—the people most directly moved by feudal ideas—seeking, through distant wars, to soothe that need for agitation which devoured them. The expedition of the Normans into Sicily, of the Burgundians into Portugal, and above all the conquest of England, are the symptoms of that restlessness with which Europe was then tormented.
