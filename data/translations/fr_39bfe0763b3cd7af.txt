The analogy between the customs of the victors and those of the vanquished was not limited to this alone. Nobility, the *colonate* [a system of tenant farming where the laborer is bound to the land], and slavery—these three great divisions of the status of persons in the Roman world—were likewise encountered among the peoples of Germania, such that religion alone established a true opposition between the two civilizations; and when Clovis bowed his head before the God of the vanquished, the fusion was not yet fully wrought, but it became easy. Indeed, we see it, following this great event, operating under the influence of simple, common ideas, so to speak, the adoption of which was commanded by necessity.

The granting of lands charged with the payment of a rent or the fulfillment of a duty is a contract that nothing distinguishes from ordinary conventions, and which has existed in all times and in almost every place. Assuredly, feudalism would not have been born from a mere stipulation of this kind. But the barbarian peoples possessed only crude institutions and rough, bellicose manners; and when the common enemy had been struck down, they turned against one another the warlike energy with which they were animated. 

The state of war became the regime of Europe. One may easily understand what must have been the position of landowners in such a social order; being either the authors or the victims of public misfortunes, they were forced to grant lands which some had not the leisure to cultivate and others were powerless to defend. The practice of such concessions spread everywhere. The proprietors felt it behooved them to demand of the grantees not a simple rent in coin, but military service, so that if the common property were one day threatened, all those who had an interest in repelling the aggression might take up arms and come to place [themselves...]
