Feudalism was not by its nature flexible, nor a friend to compromise; it must needs reign undivided or exist not at all. The Conqueror, therefore, sought not to wed feudal usages with Anglo-Saxon customs for the purpose of creating a sort of mixed government which might, by its suppleness, suit both the victors and the vanquished; he made concessions, yet these concessions were of little moment, few in number, and born of necessity. 

The revolution which in France, Germany, and Italy had unfolded slowly, century by century, and through obstacles ever-recurring, was realized systematically in England, yet with promptness and by the simplest means. The kingdom, save for the royal demesne (*domaine de la couronne*), was divided into seven hundred great baronies held directly of the King, and into seventy thousand two hundred and fifteen inferior baronies, being vassals of the former. Ecclesiastical holdings were, despite the opposition and even the revolts of their possessors, included within this organization of the soil. 

The country having been thus divided and subdivided, the Conqueror distributed lands unto his companions-in-arms, leaving a great quantity to those Anglo-Saxon lords who showed themselves favorable to his rule; but upon the one as upon the other, he imposed the obligation to hold these lands under the same conditions by which all vassals in the rest of Europe held their fiefs (*feodum*).
